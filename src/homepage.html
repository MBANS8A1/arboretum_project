Arboretum Synopsis

Welcome to the site of our picturesque arboretum located near Higham Hill, Walthomstow in London. We have an abundant collection of growing common and rare tree species as well as tree specimens that are relics of the ancient times. Feel free to inquire via about visiting the arboretum whether by yourself or with family or friends so your journey well planned in advance.

How we became to be?

Sprouty's Flourishing Arboretum was established formally in 2018 under the "Parks For London" scheme rolled out by the Mayor's Office after receiving grants and charitable donations totally in excess of £650000000. The aim of the arboretum was to create green spaces around London to reduce the amount of CO2 in our urban living spaces. Trees absorb approximately 25kg of CO2 per year so there so planting them is a strong counterbalance to climate change. We started with 14 species of trees but we aim to enlarge that by at least a quarter by the end of 2024.

A new magnolia species has been discovered in Central America

We are happy to share with you that a new species of magnolia tree was found in Pico Bonito National Park, Hondoras at the end of 2023. The tree was found clinging to a steep mountainside within the park and has been given the name Magnolia ciroorum.There are only five known specimens of the tree known in the world. We are currently scheduled to have a meeting with Sergio Estrada and Donovan Aguirre of FUPNAPIB ( members of the park's foundation ) in September 2024 in order to discuss sharing of possible tree cuttings for study.

Want to know some interesting facts about magnolias?
  • They are prehistoric being the earliest known flowering trees with fossils dating back 100 million years.
  • Since they existed before bees ever existed they relied on beetles for pollination.
  • Magnolia wood is soft and light in colour and is used to make crates, boxes and light furniture.
  • Magnolia officinalis bark is used traditional Chinese medicine to treat anxiety and improve lung health.
  • According to various myths out there white magnolia flowers symbolize purity,luck and stability,whilst pink magnolia flowers symbolize youth,joy and innocence.

Have you heard of trees that grow underground?

Two new tree species have been discovered in the Kalahari sands of Angola. They are odd for treees because the only portions of them that are visible are the flowers on the surface of the sand/soil. Their roots are adapted for nutrient-poor soils and sands. We are in contact with Kew Gardens' David Goyder about these species grow in free-draining sandy highlands about how we can contribute to the to the study of these underground trees with our dendrologists.

Cochlospermum adjanyae (Adjany's Cochlospermum)

Belongs to a genus of around 16 different species of trees and have bright yellow flowers and palmitisect leaves. There currently is not enough data yet to determine this tree's conservation status.

Baphia arenicola ('Growing on Sand')

The visible portion of this tree are white flowers wth a small triangle of yellow towards their base. This tree is in this "Least Concern" category as the grasslands it grows in and around are not under threat due to agricultural development. Although considered a tree, it is part of the bean family.

The Woven Stickman Hut

This iconic construct is made from old tree branches carefully woven together in bundles. Architect Steven Padley contributed to the design.

Mackon Arboretum Greenhouse

This is the old greenhouse will built back in 2018 to house exotic plants and trees and is used for educational trips and research purposes. Named after our head of management, George H. Mackon.

Sir Rogers woodland pond

This pond has exotic carp in it and is surrounded by various trees, shrubs and plants. Sir Roger Moore threw a £2 coin inside of the pond as a good luck to the arboretum's future in 2020.

Outside Nimox Greenhouse

This is the outside view of the Nimox greenhouse that sits in the central lawns of the arboretum. Named after Robert Nimox who died in 1851 and was notable horticulturalist in the early 19th century in East London.
